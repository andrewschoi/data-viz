It wasn't always this way.

Investor purchases in Greater Boston have risen steadily since 2004 — with sharp jumps after the 2008 financial crisis and the post-COVID real estate boom. In your neighborhood, the trend looks like this:

2008 — Financial Crisis

The foreclosure wave created a buying opportunity. Institutional investors purchased distressed properties at steep discounts, converting owner-occupied homes into rentals.

2012-2014 — Post-Crisis Surge

Wall Street-backed firms began systematically buying single-family homes as an asset class.

2020-2021 — COVID-Era Boom

Remote work drove suburban demand. Cash-rich investors capitalized on low interest rates and competed aggressively with first-time buyers.

Who gets hurt the most?

Investor activity doesn't affect everyone equally. The data shows clear patterns along lines of income and race. Lower-income households and communities of color bear a disproportionate burden — from higher eviction rates to steeper price competition.

Who owns your building?

Your eviction risk depends on who owns the building.

In Boston, corporate landlords file evictions significantly more often than small landlords. That's not because their tenants are worse — research shows the difference is driven by ownership structure, not tenant behavior.

Know your rights.

Security deposit

Landlord must hold in interest-bearing account; limited to first month, last month, security deposit, and lock change fee.

Retaliation protection

Landlord cannot evict you for reporting code violations or organizing with other tenants.

Habitability

Landlord must maintain the property to state Sanitary Code standards.

Condo conversion

If your building is being converted, you have a right of first refusal to purchase your unit and extended notice periods.

If you need help now: Contact City Life / Vida Urbana (clvu.org) or Greater Boston Legal Services (gbls.org).

The other side of the story.

We've shown you how investors can hurt. But intellectual honesty requires showing the full picture. In some cases, investor activity has positive effects: property improvements, added housing supply, and market liquidity after the 2008 crisis.

The question isn't "are investors good or bad?" — it's who benefits and who pays the cost.
