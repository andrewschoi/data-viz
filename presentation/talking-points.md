# Talking Points: Hello Homeowners Pitch

> Per-slide timing targets, key delivery phrases, and delivery notes for the 5-minute presentation.

---

## General Delivery Notes

- **Pace**: 5 minutes is tight. No slide should exceed 30 seconds. Practice with a timer.
- **Tone**: Confident but not aggressive. Let the data do the convincing. Warm when talking about people, precise when talking about numbers.
- **Transitions**: Each slide transition should feel natural. Use bridging phrases (noted below) rather than abrupt topic changes.
- **Eye contact**: Look at the audience, not the slides. The slides are visual support, not a script.
- **Numbers**: Emphasize no more than 2-3 numbers per slide. Too many statistics dilute impact.

---

## Slide-by-Slide Talking Points

### Slide 1: Title (10 seconds)

**Key phrase**: "We built an interactive experience called 'Hello, Homeowner' — a personalized data story about investor activity in Greater Boston."

**Delivery**: Brief, confident opening. State the project name and one-sentence description. Don't linger.

**Bridge to Slide 2**: "Let me start with a number that stopped us in our tracks."

---

### Slide 2: The Problem — Hook (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "One in five homes sold in Greater Boston went to an investor."
- "Not a family. Not someone looking for a place to live."
- "Nationally, investors bought 30% of homes in 2025 — a record."

**Delivery**: Pause after "one in five." Let it land. Then contrast investor vs. family to make it concrete. The national stat adds urgency.

**Bridge to Slide 3**: "But here's the part that matters most: it's not happening randomly."

---

### Slide 3: The Problem — Who Gets Hurt (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Investor activity concentrates in neighborhoods with lower home prices and larger communities of color."
- "Corporate landlords file evictions 186% more often."
- "That's not about the tenants — it's about the ownership structure."

**Delivery**: Name the neighborhoods (Roxbury, Dorchester, Mattapan) — specificity builds credibility. The 186% stat is the emotional peak of the problem framing. Pause after delivering it.

**Bridge to Slide 4**: "So who are we building this for?"

---

### Slide 4: Our Audience (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Three people. Same city. Completely different experiences."
- "Maria, James, Angela" — use first names to humanize
- "You can't tell one story. You have to tell three."

**Delivery**: Paint quick portraits of each persona. Don't read their full descriptions — summarize in one phrase each. The punchline is "you can't tell one story."

**Bridge to Slide 5**: "That's why we built a personalized experience."

---

### Slide 5: The Personalization Concept (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Before you see any data, we ask four questions."
- "Income, race, renter or owner, neighborhood."
- "Everything you see after that is filtered through YOUR answers."
- "68% more likely to be read completely."

**Delivery**: The "four questions" list should be delivered crisply. The 68% stat justifies the design decision with research. If showing a wireframe, point to the form and the persistent header bar.

**Bridge to Slide 6**: "Let me show you what the data reveals."

---

### Slide 6: Key Finding 1 — Geographic Patterns (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Investors aren't interested in Back Bay — prices are too high to profit."
- "They target two- and three-family homes in lower-income neighborhoods."
- "50% of three-family home sales went to investors by 2018."

**Delivery**: The Back Bay contrast is vivid — use it. The 50% stat for three-family homes is the most striking geographic number. If showing the choropleth, gesture at the contrast between dark and light areas.

**Bridge to Slide 7**: "And this trend is accelerating."

---

### Slide 7: Key Finding 2 — The Timeline (20 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "From 16% to 23% in just 14 years."
- "Two inflection points: the 2008 crisis and the COVID boom."

**Delivery**: Quick slide. Two sentences, two numbers, two dates. Don't over-explain — the visual (timeline chart) does the work.

**Bridge to Slide 8**: "So what does this mean for you personally?"

---

### Slide 8: Key Finding 3 — Personalized Advice (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "This is the heart of the experience."
- "Two tracks: renter and owner."
- "If you're a renter, we show you who owns your building and your eviction risk."
- "If you're a buyer, we show your price-to-income reality and where the cash buyers aren't."

**Delivery**: Emphasize that this is where the experience becomes truly personal. The split between renter and owner tracks is the design's differentiating feature. Name specific examples from each track.

**Bridge to Slide 9**: "And we don't just show you the problem — we show you what's being done."

---

### Slide 9: Supplementary Finding — Policy (20 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Massachusetts is debating historic housing reforms."
- "2026 ballot initiative — 124,000 signatures."
- "Policies ranked by relevance to YOUR situation."

**Delivery**: Fast-paced. This slide shows depth and timeliness. The ballot initiative number demonstrates real-world urgency. Don't explain each policy — just signal the breadth.

**Bridge to Slide 10**: "Let me show you how it all flows together."

---

### Slide 10: Wireframe Walkthrough (30 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Nine screens. One personalized story."
- "Martini glass narrative: we guide you through the big picture, then you explore at your own pace."
- "From landing form to personalized closing — every screen responds to your inputs."

**Delivery**: If the journey map is available, walk through it left to right with a sweeping gesture. Name the screen transitions: "Landing to Big Picture, Map to Timeline, then the split into renter and owner tracks." Don't describe every screen — highlight the flow.

**Bridge to Slide 11**: "And here's the visual design that brings it all together."

---

### Slide 11: Design Study Preview (20 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Warm and editorial — not academic."
- "Your data is always highlighted."
- "Accessible: keyboard navigation, screen reader support, reduced motion."

**Delivery**: Let the design visuals speak. Point to specific design elements (color, typography, highlight treatment). The accessibility mention signals professionalism.

**Bridge to Slide 12**: "Let me leave you with one thought."

---

### Slide 12: Closing (20 seconds)

**Key phrases**:
- "Housing is personal."
- "The same trend affects different people in completely different ways."
- "We built this so you can see your story in the data."
- "Thank you."

**Delivery**: Slow down. This is the emotional landing. "Housing is personal" — pause. The repetition of "your story" connects back to the personalization theme. End with a clear, warm "Thank you" and eye contact.

---

## Emergency Timing Adjustments

If running **over time** (cut these first):
1. Slide 7 (Timeline) — can be absorbed into Slide 6 with one additional sentence
2. Slide 9 (Policy) — can be mentioned in one sentence during Slide 8
3. Slide 11 (Design Study) — can be reduced to "we followed an editorial design approach with full accessibility"

If running **under time** (expand these):
1. Slide 4 (Audience) — tell a slightly longer story about one persona
2. Slide 10 (Wireframe) — walk through 2-3 specific screen transitions in detail
3. Slide 8 (Personalized Advice) — give a concrete example of the renter vs. owner track output

## Q&A Preparation

Likely questions and suggested responses:

**"Where does the data come from?"**
> "Primarily from MAPC's Homes for Profit report, which analyzed all residential transactions in Greater Boston from 2004-2018. We supplement with eviction court filings, Census demographics, and MassGIS geographic data."

**"How do you handle privacy with the demographic inputs?"**
> "Nothing is stored. All data stays in the user's browser. Personalization is done client-side by filtering pre-aggregated data. No individual-level data is ever used."

**"Isn't this one-sided against investors?"**
> "We have a full screen — Screen 7, 'The Other Side' — dedicated to showing where investment helps. And our policy section includes honest caveats about trade-offs. We believe credibility comes from presenting both sides."

**"How accurate are the personalized recommendations?"**
> "They're based on aggregate data, not individual predictions. We frame them as 'here's what the data says about people in your situation' rather than 'this will happen to you.' We're transparent about limitations."

**"What about the 2026 ballot initiative?"**
> "It's on track to appear on the November 2026 ballot, with over 124,000 signatures gathered. Our policy research covers the evidence for and against rent control — including the well-documented trade-offs from other cities."
