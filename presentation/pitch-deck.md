### Slide 2: The Problem — Hook (0:10 - 0:40)

**Visual**: Large animated stat on dark background. Single number dominates.

> # 1 in 5
> homes sold in Greater Boston went to an investor — not a family looking for a home.
>
> That's over 21% of all residential transactions from 2004-2018.
> And by 2025, investors are buying **30% of homes nationally** — a record high.

**Speaker notes**: "Here's a number that stopped us in our tracks. One in five homes sold in Greater Boston over the past two decades went to an investor. Not a family. Not someone looking for a place to live. An investor looking for a return. And nationally, the trend is accelerating — investors bought 30% of all homes in the first half of 2025. That's a record."

**Source callout**: MAPC "Homes for Profit" (2023); Cotality (2025)

### Slide 3: The Problem — Who Gets Hurt (0:40 - 1:10)

**Visual**: Split layout — stat on left, contextual quote/story on right.

> **It's not random. It's targeted.**
>
> Investor activity is highest in neighborhoods with:
> - Lower home prices
> - More renters
> - Larger BIPOC and immigrant populations
>
> Roxbury, Dorchester, Mattapan — the neighborhoods most affected are the ones with the deepest community roots.
>
> Corporate landlords in Boston file evictions **186% more often** than small landlords.

**Speaker notes**: "But this isn't happening everywhere equally. Investors concentrate in neighborhoods with lower home prices, more renters, and larger communities of color. Roxbury, Dorchester, Mattapan — these are the neighborhoods hit hardest. And here's the kicker: corporate landlords in Boston file evictions 186% more often than small landlords. That's not because their tenants are worse — research shows the difference is driven by ownership structure."

**Source callout**: MAPC (2023); Urban Institute eviction study

### Slide 4: Our Audience (1:10 - 1:40)

**Visual**: Three persona cards side by side.

> ## Who Is This For?
>
> **Primary audience**: Boston-area residents — renters and homeowners — who sense something is wrong with their housing market but lack the data to see the full picture.
>
> **Three personas**:
> - Maria, renter, $45K, Dorchester — worried about rent increases and eviction
> - James, first-time buyer, $95K, Somerville — losing bids to cash offers
> - Angela, homeowner, $150K, Roslindale — watching her neighborhood change
>
> **The key insight**: The same investment trend affects these three people in completely different ways. You can't tell one story — you have to tell three.

**Speaker notes**: "So who are we building this for? Boston-area residents — real people who sense that something's off about their housing situation but don't have the data to see the bigger picture. Think of three people: Maria, a renter in Dorchester, worried about a corporate landlord raising her rent. James, trying to buy his first home in Somerville but losing every bid to cash offers. Angela, watching investor-purchased buildings go up around her in Roslindale. The same trend — investor activity — affects these three people in completely different ways. That's why we built a personalized experience."

### Slide 6: Key Finding 1 — Geographic Patterns (2:10 - 2:40)

**Visual**: Choropleth map preview or wireframe.

> ## Where Investors Buy
>
> Investors dominate **30%** of two-family and **50%** of three-family home sales.
> Activity concentrated in neighborhoods with the lowest home prices and highest BIPOC populations.
> Individual flippers were **25%** likely to flip single-family homes (vs. 8% for non-investors).
>
> [WIREFRAME: Screen 3 — Investor Map — pending export]

**Speaker notes**: "Let's look at where investors are buying. They're not interested in Back Bay or Beacon Hill — prices are too high to profit. They target two- and three-family homes in lower-income neighborhoods, buying 30 to 50 percent of those sales. And when large investors buy, they flip nearly a quarter of single-family homes, compared to just 8% for regular buyers. Your choropleth map shows this pattern — darker areas mean more investor activity, and your neighborhood is highlighted."

**Source callout**: MAPC Homes for Profit (2023)
