## Renter Track

### 6A: Landlord Type Breakdown

**Core insight**: The type of landlord you have is one of the strongest predictors of your housing stability — more than your neighborhood, your income, or your lease terms.

#### Landlord Categories

| Type | Description | Typical Behavior | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Corporate / PE** | Private equity firms, REITs, large investment companies. Own 100+ units across multiple buildings. | Aggressive rent increases, high eviction filing rates, often manage via property management companies. Maintenance can be deprioritized for profit. | HIGH |
| **Medium LLC** | Small to medium investors operating through LLCs. Own 10-99 units. | Variable behavior. Some are professional and responsive; others use LLC structure to avoid accountability. Growing segment. | MODERATE-HIGH |
| **Small individual** | Individual investors who own 1-9 rental units. May or may not be local. | Often more responsive to tenant concerns. Lower eviction filing rates. More likely to negotiate. | MODERATE |
| **Owner-occupant** | Landlord lives in the building (common in 2-3 family homes). | Lowest eviction rates. Most responsive to maintenance. Personal relationship with tenants. | LOW |
| **Nonprofit / Public** | Housing authorities, CDCs, nonprofit housing organizations. | Mission-driven. Rent-restricted. Waitlists common but strong tenant protections. | LOW |

### 6B: Eviction Risk Assessment

**Core insight**: Your eviction risk is not random. It correlates with landlord type, and landlord type correlates with neighborhood and race.

#### Key Statistics

- Corporate landlords file evictions **186% more often** than small landlords in Boston (Urban Institute)

- Eviction rates are **highest** in neighborhoods with the most corporate ownership — the same neighborhoods with the highest BIPOC populations (MAPC, NYU Furman Center)

### 6C: Tenant Rights by Municipality

**Core insight**: Massachusetts provides baseline protections, but some municipalities have stronger local ordinances. Know what applies to you.

#### Statewide Protections (All MA Renters)

1. **Security deposit limits**: First month, last month, security deposit (1 month max), lock change fee. Must be held in interest-bearing account.
2. **Habitability**: Landlord must maintain property to MA Sanitary Code. You can report violations to your local Board of Health.
3. **Retaliation protection**: Landlord cannot evict you or raise rent in retaliation for reporting violations, organizing, or exercising legal rights. (MA General Laws Ch. 186, Sec. 18)
4. **Discrimination protection**: Fair Housing Act protections + MA Fair Housing Law (Ch. 151B) — cannot be denied housing based on race, color, national origin, sex, familial status, disability, religion, sexual orientation, gender identity, age, marital status, military status, or receipt of public assistance.
5. **Condo conversion protections**: Right of first refusal to purchase your unit. Extended eviction notice (5+ years) for elderly, disabled, and low/moderate-income tenants. Now applies to 2-3 family buildings (2024 Affordable Homes Act).
6. **Eviction record protection**: Old eviction records cannot be held against you when seeking new housing (2024 Affordable Homes Act).

#### Boston-Specific Protections

- **Jim Brooks Stabilization Act**: Provides relocation assistance and right of first refusal for tenants in buildings being sold, renovated, or converted.
- **Inspectional Services Department**: Report code violations at 311 or boston.gov/311.
- **Office of Housing Stability**: Provides tenant advocacy and mediation services.

#### What's Coming (Proposed)

- **Rent stabilization**: If passed, would cap annual increases at 5% and ban no-fault evictions. Could be enacted through legislation or 2026 ballot initiative.
- **Just cause eviction**: Would require landlords to have a legal reason for eviction.

#### If You Need Help Now

### 6F: Cash Buyer Competition

**Core insight**: When investors pay cash, they skip financing contingencies, appraisals, and inspections — making their offers faster and more certain. This gives them a structural advantage over mortgage-financed buyers.

#### Personalized Data

> In **[Neighborhood]**, cash buyers won **[X]%** of residential sales in the last data year. That's **[up/down] [Y] percentage points** from five years ago.
>
> **Who are these cash buyers?**
> - Corporate/PE investors: ~[X]%
> - Small LLCs: ~[Y]%
> - Individual investors: ~[Z]%
> - Non-investor cash buyers (relocators, downsizers): ~[W]%

#### Practical Tips for Financed Buyers

1. **Get fully pre-approved** (not just pre-qualified) before viewing homes. Sellers take pre-approval letters more seriously.
2. **Consider escalation clauses**: Offer to beat competing bids by a set amount, up to a maximum — shows commitment without overcommitting.
3. **Write a personal letter**: Not always effective, and some states are limiting this practice, but in MA it remains legal and can differentiate your offer.
4. **Shorten your contingency periods**: If you can complete inspections quickly, a shorter timeline makes your offer more competitive.
5. **Look at neighborhoods with lower cash-buyer rates**: See Section 6H below.

*Source: Analysis #5, cash vs. financed purchase trends by geography.*

### 6G: Rent vs. Buy Breakeven

**Core insight**: The answer to "should I rent or buy?" depends on how long you plan to stay, your local market, and your financial situation. The breakeven calculator shows when buying becomes cheaper than renting.

#### How the Calculator Works

The interactive chart plots cumulative housing costs over time for two scenarios:
- **Renting**: Monthly rent + renter's insurance, growing at the local historical rent increase rate
- **Buying**: Mortgage payments + taxes + insurance + maintenance + opportunity cost of down payment, minus equity buildup and tax benefits

The **breakeven point** is where the buying line crosses below the renting line — the year after which buying has been the cheaper option.

#### Personalized Output

> At current prices and rents in **[Neighborhood]**:
> - Median home price: **$[X]**
> - Median monthly rent (comparable unit): **$[Y]**
> - Estimated breakeven: **[Z] years**
>
> If you plan to stay more than **[Z] years**, buying is likely the better financial move. If less, renting may be more cost-effective — especially when you factor in the opportunity cost of your down payment.

#### Adjustable Parameters (slider controls)

| Parameter | Default | Range |
|---|---|---|
| Mortgage rate | Current 30-yr avg | 4%-9% |
| Down payment | 20% | 5%-30% |
| Annual home appreciation | Local historical avg | 0%-8% |
| Annual rent increase | Local historical avg | 0%-8% |
| Maintenance cost | 1% of home value/year | 0.5%-2% |

#### Income-Specific Notes

**Under $35K**:
> At this income level, the down payment barrier is likely the biggest obstacle. Even if the breakeven math works, saving for a 20% down payment on a $[X] home means $[Y] — potentially many years of saving. FHA loans (3.5% down) and first-time buyer programs may help.

**$35K-$75K**:
> The breakeven works in your favor in many neighborhoods, but the upfront costs are challenging. Look into MassHousing first-time buyer programs and community land trust options.

**$75K-$125K**:
> The math increasingly favors buying if you plan to stay 5+ years. Your main challenge is competing with cash buyers, not affordability per se.

**$125K+**:
> Buying is almost certainly favorable in the long run. The question is where — investor competition varies dramatically by neighborhood.
