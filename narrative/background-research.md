## 1. Contextual References

### Reference 1: MAPC "Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston" (2023)

- **Citation**: Metropolitan Area Planning Council. "Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston." November 2023. https://homesforprofit.mapc.org/report (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Between 2004 and 2018, 1 in 5 residential properties sold in Greater Boston were purchased by institutional investors or speculators (21% of all transactions). Investor activity was concentrated in neighborhoods with low housing prices, high renter populations, and high BIPOC and immigrant populations. Investors dominated 30% of two-family and 50% of three-family home sales by 2018. Investor purchasers were far more likely to buy with cash, purchase at a discount, and flip properties.
- **Relevance**: This is the foundational dataset and analysis for our project. It directly maps investor activity by neighborhood, property type, and demographic context in our study area.

### Reference 2: MAPC "Homes for Profit Phase 2: Corporate Ownership Networks" (2025)

- **Citation**: Metropolitan Area Planning Council. "Homes for Profit Phase 2: An Analysis of Corporate Ownership Networks." October 2025. https://www.mapc.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Homes-for-Profit-Phase-2-Final-White-Paper-2025.pdf (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Phase 2 traces the corporate ownership networks behind investor purchases, revealing how LLCs and shell companies obscure the true scale of concentrated ownership. This follow-up analysis deepens understanding of who is behind the purchases identified in Phase 1.
- **Relevance**: Supports our Investor Map screen by adding granularity to investor type categories (big corporate, PE, small LLC, individual flipper).

### Reference 3: Raymond et al., "Corporate Landlords, Institutional Investors, and Displacement" (2016, Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta)

- **Citation**: Raymond, Elora L., Richard Duckworth, Benjamin Miller, Michael Lucas, and Shiraj Pokharel. "Corporate Landlords, Institutional Investors, and Displacement: Eviction Rates in Single-Family Rentals." Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta Community & Economic Development Discussion Paper No. 2016-04. https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2893552 (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Large corporate owners of single-family rentals (15+ homes) are 8% more likely to file eviction notices than small landlords. Some of the largest private equity firms file eviction notices on a third of their properties in a single year. These differences hold after controlling for property and neighborhood characteristics.
- **Relevance**: Directly supports our "Who Gets Hurt" screen and the renter track of "Things You Should Know." Establishes that the type of landlord matters for eviction risk.

### Reference 4: Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies, "State of the Nation's Housing" (2024/2025)

- **Citation**: Joint Center for Housing Studies of Harvard University. "The State of the Nation's Housing." Annual report. https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/ (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Racial disparities in housing cost burden persist: 29% of Black renters, 21% of Hispanic renters, and 18% of Asian renters are in arrears, compared to 11% of white renters. A rising share of single-family rental conversions are purchased by large institutional investors. The white-Black homeownership gap continues to widen.
- **Relevance**: Provides the national racial disparity framing that makes our personalization-by-race meaningful. Justifies why race is a necessary input in the personalization engine.

### Reference 5: Cotality (CoreLogic), "Investors Buy Nearly One-Third of Homes Across US" (2025)

- **Citation**: Cotality. "Investors Buy Nearly One-Third of Homes Across US." 2025. https://www.cotality.com/press-releases/investors-buy-nearly-one-third-of-homes-across-us (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: A record 30% of single-family home purchases in the first half of 2025 were made by investors. In January 2025, investors accounted for 32% of purchases. Medium-sized investors (10-99 properties) grew their market share from 6% to 10% between June 2024 and June 2025.
- **Relevance**: Provides the national "Big Picture" framing stat. Shows that the Boston-area phenomenon is part of a national trend accelerating post-pandemic.

### Reference 6: NYU Furman Center, "Examining the Behavioral Differences of Corporate Landlords" (2023)

- **Citation**: NYU Furman Center. "Examining the Behavioral Differences of Corporate Landlords." https://furmancenter.org/thestoop/entry/examining-the-behavioral-differences-of-corporate-landlords (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Studies confirm that corporate, institutional, and larger landlords file more evictions and charge higher rents. Corporate landlords concentrated in neighborhoods with higher proportions of Black residents, resulting in disproportionate eviction increases.
- **Relevance**: Reinforces the racial dimension of investor impact — critical for our demographic personalization and the "Who Gets Hurt" screen.

### Reference 7: Urban Institute, "Do Large Landlords' Eviction Practices Differ from Small Landlords'?"

- **Citation**: Urban Institute / Housing Matters. "Do Large Landlords' Eviction Practices Differ from Small Landlords'?" https://housingmatters.urban.org/research-summary/do-large-landlords-eviction-practices-differ-small-landlords (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: In the Boston multifamily market, large landlords filed evictions 186% more often and medium landlords filed 55% more often than small landlords. These differences were driven by ownership structure, not tenant characteristics.
- **Relevance**: Boston-specific data that directly powers the renter advice track. This stat is one of the most striking for local personalization.

### Reference 8: GBH News, "Across Massachusetts, Shrouded Corporations Are Scooping Up Single-Family Homes" (2022)

- **Citation**: Healy, Jenifer B. "Across Massachusetts, Shrouded Corporations Are Scooping Up Single-Family Homes." GBH News, May 16, 2022. https://www.wgbh.org/news/housing/2022-05-16/across-massachusetts-shrouded-corporations-are-scooping-up-single-family-homes (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Investigative reporting documenting how corporate entities use LLCs to obscure ownership while purchasing single-family homes across Massachusetts. Details the opacity of ownership structures that make it difficult for communities and policymakers to track investor activity.
- **Relevance**: Supports the narrative framing that this is a deliberately obscured phenomenon — reinforces why data journalism and transparency matter.

### Reference 9: Scrollytelling Engagement Research (Multiple Sources)

- **Citation**: Seyser & Zeiller. "Investigating User Behavior in Slideshows and Scrollytelling as Narrative Genres in Medical Visualization." Computers & Graphics, 2023. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0097849323001061 (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Additional**: Storydoc research finding that personalized presentations are 68% more likely to be read in their entirety. https://www.storydoc.com/blog/scrollytelling-examples (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Scrollytelling consistently increases engagement, enjoyment, and perceived clarity compared to static text. Data comics and narrative visualizations produce greater recall and understanding. Scrollytelling reduces cognitive load by segmenting information into digestible, scroll-triggered chunks.
- **Relevance**: Justifies our scrollytelling format and personalization approach as a design decision rooted in engagement research.

### Reference 10: Shelterforce, "Boston Organizers Protect Individual Tenants, While Trying to Change the System" (2022)

- **Citation**: Shelterforce. "Boston Organizers Protect Individual Tenants, While Trying to Change the System." December 2022. https://shelterforce.org/2022/12/13/boston-organizers-protect-individual-tenants-while-trying-to-change-the-system/ (accessed 2026-03-08)
- **Key findings**: Documents how City Life / Vida Urbana and other Boston-area tenant organizing groups use eviction defense and collective bargaining to resist displacement by corporate landlords. Profiles the on-the-ground human experience of facing investor-driven eviction.
- **Relevance**: Provides the human stories and advocacy context for our Policy & Action and Closing screens. Grounds the data in lived experience.

## 3. Human Impact Stories

### Story 1: Lucky Omorodion and City Realty (Boston)

Lucky Omorodion had been a member of City Life / Vida Urbana since 2012. After City Realty purchased his building, the corporate landlord imposed a $300/month rent increase within months. Through collective organizing and legal action with CLVU, Lucky's family won a settlement allowing them to stay in their home with a 5-year renewable lease capped at 3% annual increases. This story illustrates both the threat of corporate acquisition and the power of organized tenant response.

*Source: City Life / Vida Urbana, https://www.clvu.org/victory (accessed 2026-03-08)*

### Story 2: East Boston Neighborhood Trust

After years of tenant organizing against displacement, City Life / Vida Urbana worked with the East Boston Community Development Corporation to create the East Boston Neighborhood Trust (EBNT), which took ownership of buildings in December 2022. This represents a community-driven alternative to corporate ownership — tenants organized to take their housing out of the speculative market entirely.

*Source: City Life / Vida Urbana & Shelterforce (accessed 2026-03-08)*

### Story 3: The 35% Rent Increase Ultimatum

A real estate investment firm purchased an apartment complex in Massachusetts and told tenants they had two weeks to agree to a 35% rent increase — or face increases of up to 52% and possible eviction. This story was cited in reporting on the 2026 rent control ballot initiative as exemplifying the abuses that current law fails to prevent.

*Source: Bay State Banner, April 2025, https://baystatebanner.com/2025/04/16/new-rent-control-legislation-takes-aim-at-drastic-increases/ (accessed 2026-03-08)*

### Story 4: Roxbury, Dorchester, and Mattapan — Targeted Neighborhoods

The MAPC Homes for Profit report documents how investor activity concentrated in lower-income neighborhoods of color — Roxbury, Dorchester, and Mattapan were among the most affected. These are historically redlined neighborhoods where Black and Latino families built community over generations. Investor speculation in these areas drives displacement from neighborhoods where residents have deep roots.

*Source: MAPC Homes for Profit, 2023 (accessed 2026-03-08)*

## 4. Why Personalization Matters

### Research Evidence

1. **68% more engagement**: Personalized presentations are 68% more likely to be read in their entirety compared to generic ones (Storydoc, 2023). This directly justifies our decision to ask users for their demographics before showing them data.

2. **Reduced cognitive load**: Scrollytelling segments information into digestible, scroll-triggered chunks, preventing the "wall of text" effect. When combined with personalization, users process only the data relevant to their situation, reducing overwhelm (Seyser & Zeiller, 2023).

3. **Greater recall**: Data comics and narrative visualizations produce greater recall and understanding of underlying data compared to text-only presentations. When the data is about the user's own neighborhood and situation, recall is further enhanced (Seyser & Zeiller, 2023).

4. **Emotional connection**: Research on narrative visualization consistently shows that personal relevance drives emotional engagement. A chart showing "eviction rates in Boston" is informative; a chart showing "eviction rates in YOUR neighborhood for people in YOUR income bracket" is motivating.

### Design Precedents

- **The Pudding** frequently uses personalization to drive engagement (e.g., "How Music Taste Evolved," asking users to input their Spotify data)
- **The New York Times** "You Draw It" series asks users to predict trends before revealing data, creating personal investment in the outcome
- **ProPublica's "Dollars for Docs"** let users search for their own doctor, transforming a national investigation into a personal one
- **The Markup's "The Landlord and Tenant"** used address-level data to personalize corporate landlord stories

### Why It Matters for This Project

Housing is inherently personal. Everyone lives somewhere. Everyone pays rent or a mortgage. By asking "What's YOUR income? What's YOUR race? Do YOU rent or own? Where do YOU live?" — we transform abstract statistics about investor activity into a mirror. The user sees themselves in the data. This is not a design gimmick; it is the core thesis of the project: **the same investment trend affects different people in radically different ways**, and you cannot understand the story without knowing who you are in it.
