## Current Proposed Legislation (2025-2026 Session)

### 1. Rent Stabilization — "An Act Enabling Cities and Towns to Stabilize Rents and Protect Tenants"

- **Bills**: Senate Bill 1447 / House Bill 2328 (194th Session, filed January 2025)
- **Also**: House Docket No. 2501
- **Status**: Under review by the Joint Committee on Housing. If advanced, could reach full House and Senate for vote in mid-2026.
- **What it does**: Enables municipalities to pass local rent control ordinances. Proposes a 5% cap on annual rent increases. Bans no-fault evictions. Requires just cause for eviction or lease non-renewal.
- **Exemptions**: Details on exemptions (new construction, small owner-occupied buildings) to be determined at the local level.
- **Source**: https://malegislature.gov/Bills/194/HD2501 (accessed 2026-03-08); Bay State Banner, April 2025, https://baystatebanner.com/2025/04/16/new-rent-control-legislation-takes-aim-at-drastic-increases/

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| Renters (all) | HIGH | Caps annual rent increases at 5%, prevents displacement |
| Low-income renters | HIGH | Most vulnerable to large rent increases; cap provides stability |
| BIPOC renters | HIGH | Disproportionately rent in investor-heavy neighborhoods with volatile pricing |
| Homeowners/buyers | LOW | Does not directly affect homeowners |
| Elderly/disabled renters | HIGH | Additional protections for vulnerable populations |

### 2. Rent Control Ballot Initiative (2026)

- **Status**: Supporters gathered over 124,000 signatures (nearly 50,000 more than required) to qualify for the 2026 ballot. On track to appear as a statewide ballot question.
- **What it does**: Limits all residential rent increases to 5% or the annual CPI increase, whichever is lower, in any 12-month period.
- **Exemptions**: New buildings (first 10 years), owner-occupied buildings with 4 or fewer units, public housing, nonprofits, short-term rentals.
- **Key context**: Massachusetts banned rent control statewide in 1994 via ballot initiative. This would be the first restoration since then. National Real Estate Advisors announced in March 2026 they would stop investing in Massachusetts because of the prospect of rent control returning.
- **Source**: WBUR, November 2025, https://www.wbur.org/news/2025/11/20/rent-control-ballot-question-massachusetts-2026-michelle-wu; Ballotpedia, https://ballotpedia.org/Massachusetts_Rent_Control_Initiative_(2026) (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| Renters (all) | HIGH | Statewide cap on rent increases |
| Low-income renters | HIGH | Greatest protection for those with least ability to absorb increases |
| BIPOC renters | HIGH | Concentrated in areas with highest rent volatility |
| Homeowners | LOW-MODERATE | May stabilize neighborhood composition; some concern about property values |
| Small landlords (owner-occupied 4 or fewer units) | EXEMPT | Carved out from requirements |

### 3. Real Estate Transfer Fee — "An Act Granting a Local Option for a Real Estate Transfer Fee to Fund Affordable Housing"

- **Bills**: H.3056 / S.1937 (194th Session)
- **Status**: Under consideration by the legislature. Nineteen communities have passed home rule petitions seeking state approval.
- **What it does**: Allows municipalities to impose a transfer fee of 0.5% to 2% on real estate transactions above $1 million (or above the county median sale price if below $750,000). Revenue funds local affordable housing development.
- **Communities with home rule petitions filed**: Arlington, Boston, Brookline, Cambridge, Concord, Chatham, Eastham, Falmouth, Nantucket, Provincetown, Somerville, Truro, Wellfleet, and others (19 total as of 2025).
- **Boston-specific**: Mayor Wu signed a transfer fee home rule petition to fund affordable housing and provide tax relief for seniors.
- **Source**: Mass. Budget and Policy Center, September 2025, https://massbudget.org/2025/09/08/testimony-real-estate-transfer-fee/; Boston.gov, https://www.boston.gov/news/mayor-wu-signs-transfer-fee-home-rule-petition-fund-affordable-housing-and-provide-tax-relief (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| Low-income renters and buyers | HIGH | Revenue funds affordable housing construction |
| BIPOC communities | MODERATE-HIGH | Affordable housing development targeted to underserved areas |
| First-time homebuyers | MODERATE | More affordable housing supply; not direct purchase assistance |
| Current homeowners (selling) | LOW-NEGATIVE | Fee applies to sellers of properties over threshold |
| Investors/flippers | MODERATE-NEGATIVE | Adds friction to speculative transactions |

### 4. Condo Conversion Protections (Expanded under Affordable Homes Act, 2024)

- **Status**: ENACTED (signed August 6, 2024 as part of the Affordable Homes Act)
- **What it does**: Expanded the 1983 Condo Conversion Law to cover two- and three-family buildings (previously only covered buildings with 4+ units). Provides tenants with:
  - Right of first refusal to purchase their unit
  - Extended eviction notice periods (5 years for elderly, handicapped, low/moderate-income tenants, extendable to 7 years)
  - Protection from retaliatory rent increases during conversion
- **Scale**: 124,400 two- and three-family buildings (287,200 homes) across Massachusetts now have protections.
- **Source**: MAPC, 2024, https://www.mapc.org/planning101/data-condo-conversion-law-changes/; Mass Legal Help, https://www.masslegalhelp.org/housing-apartments-shelter/rooming-houses-mobile-homes-condos/protections-tenants-under-state-condo (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| Renters in 2-3 family buildings | HIGH | New protections that did not exist before 2024 |
| Low-income renters | HIGH | Extended eviction notice (5-7 years) for qualifying tenants |
| Elderly/disabled renters | HIGH | Strongest protections under the law |
| BIPOC renters | HIGH | Disproportionately live in 2-3 family buildings in investor-targeted neighborhoods |
| Homeowner-occupants of 2-3 family buildings | MODERATE | Right of first refusal if converting |

### 5. Affordable Homes Act — Broader Provisions (2024)

- **Status**: ENACTED (signed August 6, 2024)
- **What it does**: The largest housing investment in Massachusetts history ($5.16 billion over 5 years). Key provisions include:
  - $2 billion for state-aided public housing capital improvements
  - $800 million for the Affordable Housing Trust Fund
  - ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit) by-right in all single-family zones statewide
  - Home inspection protection (prevents pressure to waive inspections)
  - Eviction record sealing (old eviction records cannot be held against tenants seeking new housing)
  - Creation of an Office for Fair Housing
  - Local-option real estate transfer fee (see #3 above)
- **Source**: Mass.gov, https://www.mass.gov/info-details/the-affordable-homes-act-smart-housing-livable-communities; Mass. Budget and Policy Center, October 2024, https://massbudget.org/2024/10/02/affordable-homes-act-analysis/ (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| Public housing residents | HIGH | $2B in capital improvements |
| Low-income renters | HIGH | Affordable housing fund expansion; eviction record protections |
| First-time homebuyers | MODERATE | ADU provisions increase housing supply |
| BIPOC communities | HIGH | Office for Fair Housing; targeted affordable development |
| Homeowners | MODERATE | ADU by-right allows rental income from accessory units |
| Seasonal community residents | MODERATE | New tools for seasonal housing challenges |

### 6. Just Cause Eviction Protections

- **Status**: Proposed as part of the rent stabilization bills (HD.2501, SB 1447, HB 2328)
- **What it does**: Requires landlords to have a legally recognized reason ("just cause") for evicting a tenant or not renewing a lease. Recognized causes include non-payment of rent, lease violations, owner move-in, and removal from rental market (with compliance to condo conversion laws). Prevents arbitrary no-fault evictions used to raise rents or displace tenants.
- **Source**: https://malegislature.gov/Bills/194/HD2501 (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| All renters | HIGH | Prevents arbitrary displacement |
| Low-income renters | HIGH | Most vulnerable to no-fault evictions as pretext for rent increases |
| BIPOC renters | HIGH | Disproportionately affected by no-fault evictions in investor-owned buildings |
| Elderly renters | HIGH | Stability is critical for aging in place |

### 7. Corporate Ownership Transparency / Limits

- **Status**: Under discussion; no single comprehensive bill yet. Related provisions exist in Affordable Homes Act and various home rule petitions.
- **What it does**: Various proposals aim to increase transparency of LLC ownership structures, limit the number of residential properties a single corporate entity can own, or impose additional fees/taxes on non-owner-occupied investment properties.
- **Context**: The GBH investigation (2022) documented how "shrouded corporations" use LLCs to obscure ownership. The MAPC Phase 2 report (2025) traced corporate ownership networks. Public pressure is building for transparency requirements.
- **Source**: GBH News, May 2022, https://www.wgbh.org/news/housing/2022-05-16/across-massachusetts-shrouded-corporations-are-scooping-up-single-family-homes; Boston.com, April 2024, https://www.boston.com/real-estate/local-news/2024/04/03/should-mass-ban-corporate-home-buyers/ (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Who it helps**:
| Demographic Segment | Impact Level | How |
|---|---|---|
| First-time homebuyers | HIGH | Reduces cash-buyer competition from corporate entities |
| Low-income buyers | HIGH | Levels the playing field against institutional cash offers |
| BIPOC communities | HIGH | Investor activity disproportionately targets their neighborhoods |
| Renters | MODERATE | Transparency helps identify and hold accountable problem landlords |

## Historical Policy Outcomes from Other Jurisdictions

> **Important caveat**: As the team noted, we should present these outcomes honestly. There is no perfect counterfactual — we cannot know what would have happened without the policy. These are observed correlations and researcher assessments, not proof of causation.

### Rent Control / Stabilization

**San Francisco (1994 expansion)**
- Tenants in rent-controlled units were 10-20% more likely to remain in their homes, with the effect strongest among older and long-standing residents.
- However, landlords responded by reducing rental housing supply by 15% (through condo conversions, demolitions, and conversions to non-rental use).
- Rent-regulated residents were 2.4 times more likely to face eviction proceedings than non-regulated residents.
- *Source*: Diamond, McQuade, and Qian (2019), American Economic Review; cited in D.C. Policy Center review, https://www.dcpolicycenter.org/publications/rent-control-lit-review-2025/ (accessed 2026-03-08)

**New York City (ongoing since 1947)**
- Rent regulation has been effective at maintaining below-market rents and moderating price appreciation for regulated units.
- However, benefits are not well-targeted to lower-income households — many beneficiaries are moderate- or higher-income.
- *Source*: NMHC, "Rent Regulation Policy in the United States," 2024, https://www.nmhc.org/globalassets/research--insight/research-reports/rent-regulation/rent_regulation_policy_in_the_united_states_2024.pdf (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Spain (2024 implementation)**
- Early evidence from Catalonia's rent control shows effectiveness at constraining rent growth in regulated areas.
- *Source*: arXiv, 2025, https://arxiv.org/html/2602.08631 (accessed 2026-03-08)

**Overall research consensus** (meta-review):
- Rent controls are "quite effective in terms of slowing the growth of rents paid for dwellings subject to control."
- More restrictive reforms are associated with ~10% reduction in total rental units.
- The trade-off: tenant stability vs. housing supply reduction.
- Modern "second-generation" rent stabilization (with exemptions for new construction, small landlords) attempts to mitigate supply-side effects.
- *Source*: Mense, Michelsen, and Chojna-Duch (2024), Journal of Housing Economics, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1051137724000020 (accessed 2026-03-08)

### Transfer Fees

**Multiple U.S. jurisdictions** (Aspen, CO; Park City, UT; Nantucket, MA via special legislation):
- Real estate transfer fees dedicated to affordable housing funds have successfully generated dedicated revenue streams for affordable housing production.
- Impact on transaction volume is generally modest.
- *Source*: Mass. Budget and Policy Center testimony, September 2025 (accessed 2026-03-08)

### Condo Conversion Protections

**Cambridge, MA (historical)**
- Cambridge had rent control until 1994. After its elimination, condo conversions accelerated dramatically.
- The 2024 expansion of conversion protections to 2-3 family buildings attempts to close a gap that contributed to displacement in the post-rent-control era.
- *Source*: MAPC, 2024 (accessed 2026-03-08)

## Honest Caveats for Screen 8

The following caveats should be incorporated into the Policy & Action screen to maintain credibility:

1. **No counterfactual**: "We can't predict exactly what would happen if this policy passes. No one can — because we can't run the experiment twice. But here's what the evidence from other places suggests."

2. **Trade-offs are real**: "Rent control has real benefits for current tenants. It also has documented effects on housing supply. We present both sides because you deserve the full picture."

3. **We're not lawyers**: "This is a data-informed overview, not legal advice. If you're facing an eviction or housing issue, contact one of the organizations listed below."

4. **Policies interact**: "Housing policies don't work in isolation. A transfer fee that funds affordable housing may complement rent stabilization. The effects depend on implementation details that are still being debated."

5. **Your vote matters**: "The 2026 ballot initiative means YOU get to decide on rent control. We've laid out the evidence — the choice is yours."
